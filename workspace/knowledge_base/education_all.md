📈 Best-Practice Guide on Short Options 📉 

🔘 High probability, larger capital - 70% of options expire worthless, meaning selling options automatically grants you a higher probability of making money. The trade-off for for that probability is having capital - selling puts requires you have the capital to buy the stock at the designated strike you've sold, and selling calls means you need to own 100 shares of said stock. Most newer traders purchase options as the barrier to entry is the lowest and cheapest, and this is why so many options traders lose money. By selling options, you place yourself squarely against most of retail, reaping the benefits similar to a market maker. 

STRATEGY & BEST PRACTICES

☑️ POSITION SIZING - on any strike you sell, ALWAYS plan for assignment, and not only assignment, but deep ITM assignment. Make sure you can sustain that move and always plan for the worst case scenario. This ensures you never risk more than what is wise for your portfolio, and means your assignment can become a mid-term swing and likely exit for profit vs cut at a deep loss as you're over-leveraged. Additionally while accounts can be margin-enabled, NEVER use margin to sell puts, you can multiply losses and severely damage a portfolio. 

☑️ DELTA - when looking at which delta to sell, I assess by the chart - how close are we to short term, mid term, long term support? The closer to a major support and my willingness to be assigned, the closer ATM I go. If I don't prefer assignment but favor a lower-risk swing, I judge purely by the chart instead of a set delta - which level do I think is unrealistic that price will be below by expiration? I balance as far OTM I can go while still collecting a premium that makes sense to tie up the collateral.

☑️ EXPIRATION - to capture a larger & lower-risk premium ATM, sticking to 4-6 weeks is optimal. I also favor an aggressive lower delta 1-3 week expiration strategy when you simply want to collect quick wins for less $.

☑️ COVERED CALLS - selling calls is another excellent way to benefit being on the opposite side of retail. I am far more conservative with selling CCs vs CSPs, as the market's default is up. This means you are more likely to have your shares called, especially in a bull trend. Same as puts, never sell calls that you can't accept your shares being sold at. I never close CCs for the loss, only roll for more premium. 

☑️ ASSIGNMENT - remember assignment can occur any time your strike is ITM - that being said, it becomes more likely both the closer to expiration we are and the deeper ITM price is. Additionally, strikes can always be rolled indefinitely to continue to capture more premium, and potentially delaying assignment.

🚫 RISK - we can only collect 100% of the premium we've sold for, though our losses can become exponential as the stock moves down. Theoretically, you can lose as much as the stock going to $0 after being assigned 100 shares x strike. This is the trade off - being paid simply because you're willing to buy a stock at a set price.

✅ The key to this method of trading is ALWAYS erring on the side of caution, keeping tight risk management, having a plan for the worst case scenario in every trade, and being patient for optimal levels to hit to sell options

Apex is a prop firm that allows you to trade futures in a highly-leveraged, lower risk environment by risking “their” money, allowing you to retain 90% of the profit. Only if you’re extremely successful in a PA will they switch you to a live account. Your risk is only the time and money invested into your evaluation/lifetime PA fees. Eval costs for the most popular account size - 50k are usually $36 with their frequent 80% sale. Once you build up your PA, if you blow your account, you of course lose all the progress you’ve made.

📝 Pre-market planning
0. during PM I identify potential S/R levels based on the previous HOD/LOD and possibly a 2-day old HOD/LOD. During this step I also look for supply or demand zones.
1. during PM I apply FRVP on the previous day or two depending on where the price action is. If the price action of the previous day is not relevant, i.e. not in proximity to the current day, I zoom out on the chart to find any block of relevant P/A. 
2. during PM, I look for any relevant FVG using the 5m and 15m chart.
3. the final step is done as close to market open as possible. In this step I identify call/put entry levels by taking the high/low during the entire ONS, this is the protocol defined by Z and how we determine the entry levels for the tickers on our daily published watchlists. If a previous HOD/LOD is close to these levels, I pick the highest/lowest of the levels. For example on TSLA, if the ONS had a high of 304.94, but the previous HOD was 306, then the call entry would be 306.

📝Execution
My method of day trading can be separated into two categories; 1.ORB and 2. pattern breaks. 
*ORB*: Once the market opens, I typically wait at least 6-10 minutes before I enter a trade, even if a breakout occurs. If a ticker has a large opening candle that spans my level, I don’t chase. On large moves like this an FVG is typically formed and price will tend to pull back and mitigate that imbalance of price. One of the best moves to help spot a solid breakout in price action is a break and retest of my key level. See example 2, TSLA 310C.
For breakouts, I wait for a 3 min candle to close above or below my entry level to confirm my entry along with monitoring the buying/selling volume distribution. As a rule of thumb, one should get confirmation from the time frame that is commensurate to your trade time. In my case my trades are around 10-20 minutes so I rely on the 3min and 5 min charts for confirmation.

***Pattern breaks:*** If price doesn’t break out and appears to be using my levels as S/R, I begin looking for patterns, e.g. bull/bear flags/pendants, ascending/descending wedges/channels/lines. This is where an acute understanding of price action is necessary. Trends continue until they don’t, one must take cues from the real time volume, VWAP and any S/R levels in proximity. A common theme to spot the end of a trend would be a change in price action, i.e. lower highs on an ascending trend line.

- Strategy Recap - 

Now that I have profitability data, I wanted to detail the strategy I’ve been developing over the past few months so it’s easier to understand and utilize in your own trading. @everyone 

📌 TL;DR of my strat: I use a confluence of key levels - derived of basic support/resistance, fibs, volume profile (point of control, value area high/low), trendlines/patterns, and concepts of liquidity - fair value gaps (FVGs) and swing failure patterns (SFPs), to front run breaks of market structure (MS) in order to play reversals from key levels for the most optimal risk/reward.

📌 PARAMETERS
▫️Timeframes - Can be used to play swings, day trades, and scalps
▫️Risk/Reward - must be at least 1:2+ R/R
▫️Confluence - requires 3+ for A+ setups - the best setups involving a diversity of confluences. Requires 2+ for scalps
▫️Setups - EVERY trade requires a planned (a) entry, (b) invalidation, and (c) take profit (TP) levels
▫️Scaling - optional to scale into a trade, but highly recommended to scale out. First TP away from entry, but near your planned R/R, moving stop to breakeven/in profit when possible, second TP at your planned TP, and ≤1/4th of the position as runners

📌 TA TOOLS
▫️Support/Resistance 
Basic levels are one of the most impactful tools to use in TA. Start from higher time frames (HTFs) and move to lower time frames (LTFs)
▫️Fibs
Fibs are a central component of the strategy - particularly the .5 and golden pocket (GP) (the .618 and the .65). I draw fibs on HTFs all the way to local ranges (on the 5m). I will consider any of the standard fibs as confluence, but the .5/GP are crucial to reversals. Some of the strongest confluences are stacked fibs from a variety of TFs
▫️Volume profile - session profile/fixed range volume profile 
- Point of control (POC) - the concentration of where the majority of price is trading
- Value area high & low (VAL/VAH) - 68% (one standard deviation) of where volume is trading within that range. 
I use session profiles to determine these levels on daily price action, and fixed range volume profile on many timeframes and subsections of price to find relevant inflection points

📌 LIQUIDITY & IMBALANCES
▫️Buyside/sellside liquidity (BSL/SSL) & swing failure patterns (SFPs)
I use concepts of liquidity from ICT (Inner Circle Trader), as the core thesis of how price functions; the idea being the markets will extract maximum liquidity through running stops, which also allow institutions to accumulate positions at optimal levels. I look for BSL/SSL to be taken in order to make broader reversals possible. You can find liquidity at recent and major swing lows and highs - this is where traders will place stops and attempt to breakout trade. Once that level has been beached and price has reversed, that's a swing failure pattern. SFPs occur across all timeframes, and across trendlines as well.
▫️FVGs - fair value gaps 
FVGs are imbalances in 3 candle sequences across all TFs, in which the wicks of the first and third candles do not fully overlap with the body of the middle candle, that price will want to subsequently address. I use FVGs as an additional level of confluence and potential entries for retracements with trend. When .5 of an FVG has been filled it is mitigated, and no longer draws price; when trends are strong price may only wick into them vs a complete mitigation.
